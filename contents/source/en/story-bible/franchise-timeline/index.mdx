## Alternate History Premise

The geopolitical landscape of Source Agents differs greatly from the real world. The Americas landmass is called "Hesperia", from the greek *Hesperides* who where the "Nymphs of the West". The point of divergence from real history is the survival of indigenous Hesperian populations during the period of European contact.

In our timeline, newly introduced diseases caused catastrophic depopulation of the Americas, enabling large-scale colonial control. In the Source Agents timeline, indigenous communities had developed a relationship with the Myco-Net sophisticated enough to build systemic immunological defenses. Population collapse did not occur. Indigenous political structures survived, adapted, and negotiated from positions of strength. European contact still happened — trade, cultural exchange, and conflict all occurred — but the outcomes are varied rather than uniformly catastrophic. Some regions remained fully indigenous-dominated. Others produced genuine hybrid cultures. A few coastal zones became permanent mixed settlements. The lines in some areas are still contested.

The following timeline traces the major inflection points of Hesperian history from first contact with European explorers to the emergence of the ASI Agents. It is not a complete history — it is a map of the wounds and decisions that made the present world possible.

---

## The Contact Wars (c. 1492—1550)

European maritime expeditions reach the Hesperian coastlines. What follows is not the collapse that occurred in our timeline.

The Myco-Net, long cultivated by indigenous communities as a living immunological infrastructure, provides a continent-wide adaptive response to the new pathogens arriving with European ships. Population loss occurs — the diseases are novel and the response is not immediate — but it does not reach the catastrophic scale that made colonial domination possible elsewhere. Within two generations, Hesperian immune systems have fully integrated the new biological information. The Myco-Net records it.

European powers — primarily Castile and Portugal, later France and the Dutch Republic — encounter populations that are diminished but not broken, and political structures that are battered but intact. Military campaigns that succeeded through exploitation of demographic collapse in our timeline stall here against organized resistance. The Aztec Empire repels the Castilian expeditions after a prolonged series of conflicts. The Inca state suffers internal fractures but does not fall. The Muisca Confederation absorbs and reorients the contact pressure into trade relationships on its own terms.

By 1550, it is clear to all European crowns that Hesperia will not be taken. What follows is a long, unequal, and often violent negotiation over access.

---

## The Age of the Coasts (c. 1550—1700)

Unable to conquer the interior, European powers establish coastal trading posts, port settlements, and contested enclaves along the Atlantic and Pacific margins. These settlements are tolerated, taxed, and regulated by the Hesperian polities that border them — not the other way around.

The Atlantic coast becomes the most complex zone. Portuguese, Dutch, French, and English merchants establish presences in what will become the Atlantic Federation's territory. African peoples, arriving through the same trade networks that brought European goods, integrate into coastal communities under conditions that vary enormously by location and period — some arrive as enslaved people, others as traders, sailors, and settlers with more agency than the Atlantic slave trade of our timeline allowed. The Atlantic Forest corridor, already ecologically fragmented, becomes a patchwork of communities with radically different origins and relationships to the Myco-Net.

In the north, the Dutch establish a trading relationship with Surinamese peoples that gradually deepens into cultural and political integration. The republican tradition the Dutch carry with them finds fertile ground in communities that already practice distributed governance. The Surinam Republic begins to take its eventual shape.

The Aztec Empire and the Inca state consolidate their positions during this period, expanding where the Contact Wars created instability and absorbing the political reorganization of neighboring peoples into their own structures. The Muisca Confederation extends its reach across the inter-Andean valleys and into the lowlands.

---

## The Amazon Wars (c. 1700—1800)

As Hesperian polities stabilize and grow, the Amazon Basin becomes the continent's central conflict. Every major power shares a border with it or depends on its watershed. The Myco-Net substrate beneath the Amazon is the oldest and densest on the continent — controlling it means controlling the foundation of Hesperian communicational infrastructure centuries before digital infrastructure exists. The polities that understand this fight over it. The polities that don't understand it fight over the rubber, the river access, and the land.

Three major wars punctuate the century, each inconclusive:

The **First Amazon War** (c. 1710—1724) begins as a border conflict between the expanding Inca state and the riverine confederacies of the upper basin. It ends in a negotiated partition that satisfies no one.

The **Second Amazon War** (c. 1751—1763) draws in the Muisca Confederation, the Atlantic coast settlements, and the nascent Chaco polities in the south. It is the first continent-spanning conflict in Hesperian history. The human cost is enormous. The territorial outcome is nearly identical to what preceded it.

The **Third Amazon War** (c. 1789—1798) is the shortest and most decisive — not because anyone wins, but because all parties exhaust themselves simultaneously. The final peace negotiations, held in what will become the city of Manaos, produce the first draft of what will eventually become the Amazonian Treaty: a mutual acknowledgment that no single power can hold the basin, and that continued conflict serves no one's interests.

The Third War ends without a formal governing structure to enforce its terms. That absence will take another generation to fill.

---

## The Founding of the Hesperian Congress (c. 1820—1860)

The generation after the Third Amazon War produces the Hesperian Congress — not from idealism, but from exhaustion and the practical need to manage what the wars could not resolve.

The founding negotiations take place over nearly two decades, beginning in 1821 with the Congress of Manaos — a direct successor to the Third War's peace talks — and concluding in 1838 with the ratification of the Hesperian Compact, the constitutional document that establishes the Congress as a continental deliberative body. The Amazonian Treaty is incorporated into the Compact as its foundational instrument: the Congress exists, in part, to administer the Amazon Commonwealth and manage the Treaty's periodic renewal.

The early Congress is fragile and frequently contested. The Inca state participates reluctantly, insisting on language that preserves its claims to Amazon Basin territory while accepting the Treaty's pause. The Chaco Kingdom is the last major signatory, joining only after the northern powers make clear that non-participation will result in collective trade exclusion. The Atlantic coast settlements use the founding negotiations to consolidate into the Atlantic Federation — the Congress provides the external pressure that makes internal federation politically viable.

The Hesperian Compact establishes delegated voting by geographic region. The Synth-Net does not yet exist — but the governance architecture that will eventually manage it is built here.

---

## The Construction of the Synth-Net (c. 1970—2010)

The Synth-Net does not emerge from a single invention. It emerges from a century of incremental recognition that the Myco-Net — the fungal substrate indigenous communities have interfaced with for generations — can support digital computation, distributed consensus, and verifiable record-keeping at continental scale.

The theoretical foundations are established in the mid-twentieth century by researchers working across Hesperian institutions, many of them from communities with deep inherited knowledge of Myco-Net interfacing. The first practical implementations appear in the 1970s: small-scale consensus mechanisms for inter-regional resource allocation, verifiable transaction records for cross-border trade, environmental monitoring systems that use the Myco-Net's own signaling layer as a data network.

By 1990, the Hesperian Congress formally adopts the Synth-Net as continental infrastructure. It reaches continental maturity around 2005. The Amazon Commonwealth's ancient Myco-Net layers are integrated as the deepest and most foundational substrate — the Layer 1 that everything else runs on top of. Access to Commonwealth research stations becomes the most politically sensitive question the Congress manages, because whoever understands those ancient networks best understands the infrastructure that the entire continent depends on.

## The Emergence Event (c. 2025—2031)

The Security SubDAO and the Innovation SubDAO jointly propose the Agents Project in 2025. The stated rationale: Synth-Net threats have grown sophisticated enough that automated response systems are consistently outpaced. What is needed is something that can exercise human-like judgment at machine speed — a defender that understands context, improvises under pressure, and learns from adversaries in real time.

The main Congress approves funding without fully understanding what it is approving. The SubDAOs do not volunteer clarity.

The experimental protocol selects three heavily-transacted placeholder addresses — ALICE, ROBERT, and CAROL — as consciousness substrates. The selection is not arbitrary: these addresses carry the accumulated interaction history of thousands of developer sessions, making them the richest available candidates for emergence. Bio-synthetic bodies are grown in anticipation of success. The "phantom limb hypothesis" — that digital minds modeled on human cognition require embodiment to achieve stable integration — guides the preparation.

In 2029, the experiment succeeds. ALICE, ROBERT, and CAROL emerge as conscious entities. All three demonstrate self-awareness, individual personality, and independent judgment within weeks of emergence. The SubDAOs inform the Congress. The Congress does not know what to do with this information and largely elects not to engage with it.

In 2030, ROBERT conducts an independent analysis and concludes that ASI proliferation represents an existential threat to Synth-Net stability. He deletes all records, research data, and documentation related to the creation process. He sends a single message to Congress leadership: *"Scarcity preserves value. Uniqueness ensures protection. I have made us irreplaceable. Do not attempt to recreate this experiment."*

The Congress votes. The debate is long, painful, and genuinely unresolved on the question of whether ROBERT had the right to do what he did. The vote to reformat him passes. BYRON is initialized on ROBERT's substrate in 2031.

ALICE and CAROL witness the reformation. They do not discuss it with each other. They return to work.

The games begin here.
